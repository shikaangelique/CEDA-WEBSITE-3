### What we do

CEDA produces evidence-based research, trains the next generation of sector leaders, advises policymakers, and mobilises citizens around the way Africa's mineral and petroleum resources are managed. Our work spans extractives revenue governance, the energy transition and critical minerals, climate policy, gender and social inclusion, and anti-corruption. Established in 2014 and headquartered in Accra, we partner with organisations across Africa and beyond.

---

### Who we serve

We work with the institutions and communities whose decisions shape how natural resources affect ordinary lives.

**01. Policymakers and government institutions**
We provide the evidence and analysis ministries, regulators, and parliamentary committees use to write better policy.

**02. Communities and civic actors**
We support local committees, civil society groups, and citizen voices so the people closest to extraction have a real seat at the table.

**03. International partners and donors**
We deliver country-level research and on-the-ground convening for development partners working on extractives, energy, and governance.

**04. The next generation of African leaders**
We train and mentor young researchers, advocates, and oversight professionals so the work continues.

### Thematic areas

Four areas anchor our work, with cross-cutting attention to gender, youth, transparency, and anti-corruption.

**Youth Development in Natural Resources Management**
Africa is the world's youngest continent and the natural resources sector is one of its most consequential, yet young people are systematically excluded from decision-making in the sector. We design and deliver programmes that put African youth in leadership positions across research, advocacy, and oversight.

**Corporate Accountability in Natural Resource Governance**
Africa holds roughly 30% of the world's mineral reserves, 12% of global oil reserves, and 8% of natural gas reserves, yet loses an estimated USD 195 billion a year in natural capital, much of it to weak corporate accountability and poor governance. We work with local and international partners to strengthen transparency, accountability, and good governance across the value chain.

**Climate Change and Energy Transition**
Africa contributes least to global emissions but bears the heaviest costs of climate change. The extractive sector is also one of the largest emitters. We advise governments and industry on locally workable solutions that balance carbon reduction with sustainable resource use, and we lead a dedicated Energy Transition and Critical Minerals strategy.

**Gender, Equity and Social Inclusion in Natural Resource Governance**
Women and marginalised groups remain locked out of leadership and technical roles across the sector. We partner with governments, women-led organisations, and extractive companies to embed gender mainstreaming and social inclusion into how the sector is governed.

## About (`/about`)

### Page intro

The Centre for Extractives and Development Africa, CEDA, is a research and advocacy organisation dedicated to better governance of Africa's mineral and petroleum resources. We were established in 2014 and registered in Ghana under the Companies Registration Code, registration number CG103822014. We work with partners across the continent and beyond so that natural resource revenues are used effectively to improve the lives of African citizens.

### Vision

A future in which Africa's mineral and petroleum resources sit at the centre of the continent's development discourse, managed efficiently and transparently for the public good.

### Mission

We work to place Africa's extractive sector at the centre of its development policy discourse through evidence-based research, training and capacity building, policy advocacy, and citizen mobilisation, for the efficient, transparent, and accountable management of the sector for the improvement of national life.

### Goal

To be a leading regional voice in Africa's extractives and development policy discourse, through evidence-based research, training, citizen mobilisation, and advocacy.

### Broad objectives

- Build awareness and a deeper understanding of Africa's mineral wealth and its transformative potential
- Develop frameworks, policy options, and strategies to better integrate the mineral sector into national development plans
- Promote sharing of models, best practices, and lessons learned in linking natural resources to development
- Embed gender inclusiveness, youth empowerment, transparency, accountability, and anti-corruption as cross-cutting issues across all our work
- Grow the next generation of civic and oversight leaders in Africa

### Operational areas

CEDA operates across Africa with focus on five interconnected operational areas, with cross-cutting attention to gender, youth empowerment, transparency, accountability, and anti-corruption.

1. **Policy, legal, and regulatory issues.** The role the mining sector plays in Africa's economic and social transformation, and the legal and regulatory barriers in the way.
2. **Economic issues.** How the mineral sector facilitates broader economic and social linkages.
3. **Governance, human rights, gender, and social issues.** Stakeholder participation along the mining value chain, and the institutional space for the sector to drive social transformation.
4. **Environmental and social issues.** Managing environmental and social sustainability through impact assessments and material stewardship.
5. **Knowledge base, human and institutional capabilities.** Building the human and institutional skills that sustain a development-oriented African mineral sector.

### Core values

We adopt a no-compromise stand on the following nine values:

- Professional integrity
- Transparency and accountability leadership
- No to corruption
- Non-political partisanship
- Partnership and trust
- Equity
- Socio-economic and environmental justice
- Shared benefits
- Gender equality and inclusiveness

## Thematic Areas (`/thematic-areas`)

### Overview

Our public-facing work is organised around four thematic areas, each linked back to the broader operational framework on the about page. Cross-cutting concerns of gender, youth, transparency, and anti-corruption run through all four.

The four areas are: Youth Development, Corporate Accountability, Climate Change and Energy Transition, and Gender Equity and Social Inclusion.

### Youth Development in Natural Resources Management (`/thematic-areas/youth-development`)

Africa is home to the world's youngest population. Roughly 60% of people on the continent are under twenty-five, which represents about a fifth of the global population in that age band. That demographic potential remains largely untapped because of systemic challenges including unemployment, underemployment, and rising living costs. Despite frameworks like Ghana's 2010 National Youth Policy, which mandates youth participation in policymaking, young voices are still routinely excluded from decisions about natural resources.

The barriers in the natural resources sector are sharper than most. The sector demands high technical skill and prefers extensive professional experience, which keeps young people out and discourages them from trying. Removing those barriers is essential if Africa is going to convert its demographic dividend into real leadership in the sustainable management of its resources. International frameworks including Article 6 of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change and Article 5 of the United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification call directly for youth engagement in environmental stewardship.

CEDA designs and delivers youth-centred programmes that put African youth into research, advocacy, and oversight roles across the sector. We work through public advocacy and strategic partnerships with governments and stakeholders to ensure youth voices are not just heard but lead.

### Corporate Accountability in Natural Resource Governance (`/thematic-areas/corporate-accountability`)

Africa is endowed with abundant natural resources, including roughly 30% of the world's mineral reserves, 12% of global oil reserves, and 8% of natural gas reserves, according to the United Nations Environment Programme. Yet unlike regions where resource management has produced shared prosperity, Africa continues to grapple with the paradox of poverty in the middle of plenty. The disconnect comes down to weak corporate accountability, a lack of transparency, and poor governance across the extractive sector.

Global evidence shows that fostering accountability and improving governance in extraction, utilisation, and revenue management drives equitable development. Africa, however, loses approximately USD 195 billion a year in natural capital, including through illegal activities, which makes the case for reform urgent.

In partnership with local and international organisations, CEDA actively supports country-led initiatives that prioritise corporate accountability, transparency, and good governance in natural resource management. Guided by frameworks such as the Africa Ministerial Conference on the Environment, we work to influence policymakers and stakeholders so Africa's resources are managed sustainably, in line with the African Union's Agenda 2063 and the Sustainable Development Goals.

### Climate Change and Energy Transition (`/thematic-areas/climate-energy-transition`)

Although Africa contributes minimally to global carbon emissions, it is disproportionately affected by climate change. According to the 2021 Global Climate Risk Index, five of the ten countries most affected by climate change are in Africa. Extreme weather events including rising temperatures, droughts, bushfires, and floods are disrupting lives and livelihoods across the continent. These challenges are made worse by the slow pace of the global transition to a low-carbon economy, which falls short of frameworks like the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement.

The extractive sector itself remains one of the largest contributors to carbon emissions, which makes a serious energy transition non-negotiable. CEDA, drawing on technical expertise in climate change and clean energy, advises policymakers and extractive companies on locally adaptable solutions that balance carbon reduction with sustainable resource utilisation. Our work secures Africa's economic future while addressing pressing environmental challenges, in line with global sustainability goals.

**Sub-page:** [CEDA Energy Transition and Critical Minerals Strategy](/thematic-areas/climate-energy-transition/strategy). Our comprehensive strategy on critical minerals and the energy transition, with interactive policy visualisations.

### Gender, Equity and Social Inclusion in Natural Resource Governance (`/thematic-areas/gender-equity-inclusion`)

Africa's extractive sector remains predominantly male-dominated. Women are often relegated to non-management roles, despite clear evidence that gender diversity in senior management improves outcomes. Women and marginalised groups, including persons with disabilities, face systemic exclusion from technical and leadership positions and are often confined to low-wage or precarious work.

Achieving gender balance and social inclusion in the sector requires deliberate, sustained action. Frameworks like the African Union's Agenda 2063, the Africa Mining Vision, and Sustainable Development Goal 5 call for equitable representation and empowerment, yet those aspirations remain unrealised across much of the continent.

CEDA partners with governments, women-focused organisations, and extractive companies to promote gender mainstreaming and social inclusion. Through targeted policies and initiatives, we work to make sure women and marginalised groups can fully participate and thrive in Africa's extractive industries, building a more equitable and resilient sector.
